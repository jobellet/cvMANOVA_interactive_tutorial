Tutorial 03

The Geometry of Noise

Understand why fMRI noise is structured, and how spatial prewhitening reveals the hidden signal.

Spatial Structure of Noise

Noise in fMRI is rarely "white" (independent). Neighboring voxels often share noise sources like:

  • Vascular pulsations: Heartbeat-driven changes.
  • Respiratory cycles: Breathing-induced field shifts.
  • Anatomy: Some voxels are inherently noisier due to tissue type.

This creates spatial covariance (\(\Sigma\)), meaning the noise is correlated across voxels rather than being purely random.

The Euclidean Problem

Euclidean distance weighs all voxels equally. When voxels are correlated, the noise "spreads out" along specific axes, burying the signal difference and causing standard distance metrics to fail.

The cvMANOVA Solution

Mahalanobis distance utilizes the inverted noise covariance matrix (\(\Sigma^{-1}\)) to perform **spatial prewhitening**. This effectively stretches or squashes the space to make noise spherical.

\[D^2 = (\mu_1 - \mu_2)^T \Sigma^{-1} (\mu_1 - \mu_2)\]
