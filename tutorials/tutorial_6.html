Case Study: Allefeld & Haynes (2014)

The foundational architecture of modern cvMANOVA searchlights.

Step 1: Why Standard MANOVA Fails

Classical MANOVA statistics like the Bartlett-Lawley-Hotelling trace ($T_{BLH}$) are biased estimators. As the number of voxels ($p$) increases, noise alone inflates the result.

Bias Level: High

Interactive Hint: Move the slider to see how the Purple Line (MANOVA Estimate) climbs further away from the Blue Dashed Line (True Effect = 0), even when there is no actual signal.

Step 2: The Introduction of Cross-Validation

By using a leave-one-run-out framework, cvMANOVA ensures the metric is unbiased. The training weights from one set of runs are applied to a completely independent test run.

$$\hat{D} \approx \text{trace}(\hat{B}_{train} \cdot \mathbf{C} \cdot \hat{B}_{test}' \cdot \Sigma^{-1})$$

The sampling distribution is now perfectly centered at zero under the null hypothesis. This allows us to use standard Z-scoring or permutation testing for reliable inference across participants.

Step 3: Searchlight & Boundary Standardization

Spherical searchlights often contain fewer voxels ($p$) at the brain's boundary. Since variance scales with $p$, we must divide by $\sqrt{p}$ to maintain a uniform map.

$$\hat{D}_s = \frac{\hat{D}}{\sqrt{p}}$$
Local Voxel Count ($p$): 123
Status: Steady State

Interactive Task: Drag the sphere to the edge of the brain. Watch $p$ drop and see how the standardization logic compensates for the change in sampling variance.

Without this $\sqrt{p}$ correction, the final map would show false "clusters" at the boundaries simply because of the math of the searchlight sampling.

Citation

Allefeld, P., & Haynes, J. D. (2014). Searchlight-based multi-voxel pattern analysis with cross-validated MANOVA. NeuroImage, 89, 345-357.
